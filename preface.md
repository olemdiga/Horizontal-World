r# Preface

The sky is empty. But the ground is full.

For many people today, the old vertical frameworks — a creator God, a heavenly realm, a final judgment, an afterlife that gives every action ultimate weight — no longer hold. The ceiling that once seemed to support meaning, morality, and hope has become difficult to believe in. When that happens, a familiar anxiety often follows: if there is nothing above, does anything matter?

This book argues that the collapse is not inevitable.

There have always been traditions that never asked the sky to bear such weight. They found rich sources of meaning horizontally — in relationships, rituals, roles, nature, daily practice, and attentive engagement with ordinary life. Meaning, in these worlds, does not descend from above. It grows sideways from the ground we share.

This is not a lesser form of meaning. It is a different architecture. One that does not depend on a single cosmic pillar but on a dense network of connections, cultivated habits, and embodied attention. When the vertical is removed, these horizontal structures do not necessarily crumble. They stand on their own logic.

The ground, it turns out, can be full.

---

**Horizontal World** explores what such worlds look like when taken seriously. It draws especially from East Asian traditions — Confucianism, Daoism, and Chan/Zen — while also examining parallel immanent approaches in other cultures. The book examines both their promise and their real shadows: the strength of ritual and cultivation alongside the risks of conformity and stagnation; the beauty of immanent presence alongside the challenges of modern, mobile, urban life; the possibility of ethics and dignity without a divine foundation.

This is not a polemic against vertical traditions. Those traditions have inspired extraordinary art, moral courage, and human hope. Nor is it a conversion manual. It is an exploration — a garden of ideas rather than a finished cathedral.

The essays can be read in any order. Some readers will start with the foundational contrasts. Others will begin with the East Asian roots or the modern applications. Follow the path that interests you. The book is designed for sideways movement.

It is written for anyone who can no longer believe in the ceiling but still wants the ground to be meaningful. For those who sense that life can be rich, moral, and deeply felt without needing a transcendent anchor. For those who are willing to walk without constantly looking upward for rescue or final justification.

The sky is empty.

But the ground remains — full of relationships, responsibilities, beauty, memory, and the quiet accumulation of meaningful action across time.

Let us walk sideways.

*Olem Diga*
