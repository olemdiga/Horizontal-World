# Peterson’s Vertical Anxiety

## Why a psychologist who sees belief as essential cannot fully commit to it

Jordan Peterson is the vertical world’s most articulate contemporary defender. He warns that without the structuring myths and moral framework of Christianity, Western society will descend into chaos, nihilism, and ideological possession. "If God does not exist, everything is permitted," he echoes from Dostoevsky. Humans need a "highest value" or transcendent anchor, or they become lost.

Yet when directly asked whether he himself is a Christian, Peterson refuses a clear answer. He has said he is "not, in fact, a Christian" while simultaneously insisting that acting *as if* God exists is a necessary psychological axiom. His most famous formulation captures the tension perfectly: "I act as if God exists and I am terrified that He might."

Peterson is not a hypocrite to be mocked. He is a revealing case study: a profoundly vertical thinker who has glimpsed the emptiness of the sky and cannot bear it, yet who also cannot bring himself to believe in the ceiling he insists is necessary. He is the apostle of a faith he cannot profess.

---

## Belief as Pragmatic Necessity

Peterson’s defense of Christianity is not primarily that it is *true*. It is that it is *useful* — psychologically and socially indispensable. Without God and immortality, he argues, "such things have no ultimate purpose, value, or significance." On atheism, nothing ultimately matters. The symphony, the friendship, the moral choice — all dissolve into the same meaningless dust.

This is a pragmatic, therapeutic argument for the vertical. Belief is a tool, a necessary fiction, a crutch to prevent collapse. Peterson, as a clinical psychologist, has seen many patients pulled from darkness by religious belief. He has witnessed how the loss of that belief can lead to despair, addiction, and chaos. His vertical anxiety is not abstract. It is rooted in lived clinical experience.

---

## The Shadow of the Gulag

Peterson’s fears are also shaped by Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, whose *The Gulag Archipelago* he frequently cites. For Peterson, the Soviet experiment was atheism made state policy — a society that tore down the vertical ceiling by force and unleashed hell on earth. Solzhenitsyn becomes the witness who saw the abyss and concluded that only transcendent belief could hold the darkness at bay.

This genealogy is powerful, but limited. It reflects a specific 20th-century trauma: revolutionary atheism as totalitarian ideology. It does not engage with traditions that never relied on that particular ceiling in the first place.

---

## The Evasion That Cannot Hold

Peterson’s position — "I act as if God exists" — is ultimately unstable. It is not the quiet confidence of faith, nor the honest clarity of disbelief. It is the dread of someone who has seen belief function as the only bulwark between his patients and the void, and who fears the same fate for himself and for Western civilization.

He cannot commit to belief because intellectual honesty forbids it. He cannot commit to disbelief because the clinical and historical evidence he has internalized forbids it. The result is a permanent limbo: preaching the necessity of a ceiling he cannot honestly affirm, while warning that without it the world burns.

---

## What Peterson Reveals — and Misses

Peterson is valuable to this notebook not because he is wrong, but because his struggle illuminates a deep truth about the vertical world. For those formed by it, the threat of nihilism is not merely philosophical — it is visceral and psychological. He has seen belief work as a coping mechanism that saves lives. He has read Solzhenitsyn and concluded that removing the vertical without replacement is dangerous.

But he is wrong to conclude that no robust alternative exists. He underestimates (or is simply unfamiliar with) horizontal traditions that have sustained meaning, morality, and social order for centuries without a strong transcendent ceiling. Confucian role ethics, Daoist flow, Zen immanence, African ancestor veneration, and indigenous relational cosmologies all demonstrate that the ground can be full without needing a sky to prop it up.

Peterson cannot see this path clearly because his imagination, rich in biblical archetypes, lacks deep engagement with these horizontal frameworks. He has studied the Gulag but not the tea ceremony. He has read Solzhenitsyn but not Zhuangzi. His vertical anxiety is real and deserves respect, but its cure lies not in a ceiling he cannot honestly affirm, but in a ground he has never learned to trust.

> **The vertical crutch props up those who fear the fall. But the ground does not need propping. It needs walking.**
