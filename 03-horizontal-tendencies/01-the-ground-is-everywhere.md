# The Ground is Everywhere

## Indigenous American, African traditional, and other immanent worldviews as parallel horizontals

The horizontal world is not unique to East Asia. Wherever humans have sought meaning, similar patterns appear: relationships, rituals, reciprocity with nature, continuity with ancestors, and attention to the sacred ordinary. East Asian traditions simply wrote these patterns down, systematized them, and scaled them into enduring civilizations. Elsewhere, the same impulses often live in oral traditions, ceremonies, and local cosmologies that outsiders have sometimes dismissed as primitive.

This essay notes a few of those parallels. It does not claim expertise in any of them. It claims recognition: the horizontal impulse is a recurring human response to the need for meaning without a ceiling.

---

## Indigenous North America: The Sacred as Relationship

Many Indigenous North American traditions see the sacred as present and relational. The Lakota phrase *Mitakuye Oyasin* ("all my relations") expresses a core idea: the world is full of persons — human and nonhuman. Buffalo, eagle, river, stone, and ancestor all have agency and deserve respect.

There is often a Great Spirit or Creator, but this figure is rarely the commanding, intervening God of Abrahamic traditions. In many stories the Creator sets the world in motion and then withdraws, leaving humans and other beings to maintain balance through reciprocity and ceremony. Rituals such as the sweat lodge, sun dance, or Haudenosaunee thanksgiving address are not ways to earn salvation. They renew relationships, restore harmony, and remind participants that they belong to a living web. The ground itself is sacred. There is no need to escape it.

---

## Traditional Africa: Ancestors and Immanence

Across much of sub-Saharan Africa, traditional worldviews show a similar pattern. A high God often exists but is distant — a creator who has stepped back from daily affairs. The active spiritual world is horizontal: ancestors, land spirits, and living elders. Ancestor veneration is not worship of distant deities. It is the continuation of family. The dead remain part of the community, offering guidance and receiving respect through offerings and memory.

Ritual here functions as social and cosmic maintenance. Drumming, dancing, festivals, and life-cycle ceremonies reinforce bonds, mark transitions, and keep the world in balance. Morality is not primarily obedience to divine law but alignment with community, ancestors, and the natural order. The ground is full of relationships that stretch across time.

---

## The Withdrawn Creator

A recurring figure across many horizontal traditions is the "withdrawn creator." The high God creates the world and then steps back, leaving human beings, spirits, and natural forces to manage their own affairs. This pattern appears in parts of Africa, Indigenous North America, and elsewhere. It is an elegant horizontal solution: it keeps the poetry of origin without the burden of constant divine interference or judgment.

The real work of life is not vertical obedience but horizontal maintenance — keeping good relations with the living, the dead, the land, and the community.

---

## Common Horizontal Features

These traditions, despite their differences, tend to share several features:

- **Immanence**: The sacred is here — in the land, the community, the ancestors, the body — rather than in a separate heavenly realm.
- **Reciprocity**: Relationships are mutual. Humans give respect and offerings; the land, spirits, and ancestors give support and guidance.
- **Ritual as maintenance**: Ceremonies keep the world balanced and the community connected. They do not earn salvation.
- **Embeddedness**: These systems are usually local and relational rather than universalist. Obligations apply to those within the web of relations.

---

## A Note of Caution

It is easy for outsiders to romanticize these traditions. Indigenous and African societies have their own shadows: rigid hierarchies, harsh social controls, and internal conflicts. The weight of ancestors and community can be heavy. None of these cultures are utopias. Respect requires acknowledging both their wisdom and their human limitations.

---

## Why This Matters

The presence of horizontal patterns across unrelated cultures shows that horizontality is not a cultural quirk of East Asia. It is a recurring human solution to the problem of meaning. When people are not required to look upward for a ceiling, they often build meaning sideways — through relationship, ritual, reciprocity, and careful attention to the ground they share.

The sky is not required. The ground is everywhere. You only have to learn to see it.

> **No sky required. No exception. The ground holds, wherever you stand.**
